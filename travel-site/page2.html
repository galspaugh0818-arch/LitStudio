Exploring the Udvar-Hazy Center

While inside the hangar bay, I had the chance to walk past the SR-71 Blackbird, and seeing it up close was unreal. The aircraft is so massive that you literally can’t fit the whole thing into your field of view unless you step far back. Since there was still some time before the airshow outside began, I explored the hangar a bit more. Everywhere you look—above, below, or across the floor—there’s another historic aircraft waiting to grab your attention. I remember standing in front of the Enola Gay, then wandering past fighter jets like the Typhoon, the F-4 Phantom, and even a German Bf-109 from World War II. There was so much to see that you almost feel rushed trying to take it all in before the event starts.

When I finally went outside, my first stop was the smaller Cessna-style aircraft lined up along the field. One of the owners was sitting next to his plane and was kind enough to let us climb inside. Once I sat in the cockpit, I realized just how tight the space is — you barely have room to move your arms or turn your head. What shocked me most was how light these planes are; you could actually push one by hand if you wanted to. You also have to duck under the wing unless you enjoy hitting your head. There were probably a hundred little aircraft scattered around the field, each one different.

I also spotted the Airbus A300 parked off to the side. I really wanted to get inside, but the line was insanely long, so I decided it was better to explore more instead of standing still for twenty minutes. Walking around the field with engines roaring in the background, the heat bouncing off the pavement, and helicopters kicking dust into everyone’s eyes made the whole experience feel alive. Every corner had something new to look at, and the entire day felt like stepping into aviation history brought to life.
